Wislandic is not written with the Latin alphabet, rather it has its own, native script. But for use with digital technologies and to aid in communications with those outside of Wisland, an official romanization system has been devised.

Most sounds are written with one letter, but there are digraphs and trigraphs.

Wislandic also has distinctive gemination. In its script, it has a specific letter that indicates the following consonant is geminated. While writing the consonant twice indicates it is uttered twice. In the romanization, writing two consonants next to each other indicates a geminated consonant. And should a rare double uttered consonant need to be transcribed, then an apostrophe should be used to separate them.

> [!Ex 1]
> *hina*   - /hinä/
> *hinna*  - /hinːä/
> *hin'na* - /hin.na/


[^1]: Wislandic has no voiced labial plosive /v/ or voiced dental fricative /ð/ natively. But has created letters to represent those sounds in foreign names and borrowed words. They are usually pronounced as their unvoiced counterpart.
